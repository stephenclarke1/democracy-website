How to Enrol and Vote

Learn about how voting works in New Zealand and the MMP system.

How Voting Works

In 1993, New Zealand switched to the MMP system of voting, from the FPP (First-past-the-post) system. This was in an effort to make the democratic system more representative of New Zealanders' views.

FPP

Under the old FPP system, everyone eligible to vote would get 1 vote, where they would vote for the person to represent their area in parliament, so all seats were electorate seats. The candidate with the most votes is elected to parliament.

In this example, Hairy Maclary has recieved 100 votes, more than any of the other candidates, so is elected to parliament.

There are some possible problems with this system

In the above example, Hairy Maclary was elected with only 37.5% of the vote, with the majority of voters favouring other candidates. If this were in a safe Hoiho Party seat, the 125 voters who did not vote for Mr Maclary have no other way of having a say in the make-up of parliament.

Critics of this system also say it favours a two-party system, and excludes minor parties. If the Kiwi Party consistently recieved 15% of the votes in all of the electorates, they would have recieved 15% of the popular vote (the total votes in the election), but no representation in parliament.

MMP

MMP sought to make the share of seats in parliament more reflective of the popular vote, and is our current system of electing MPs.

Under MMP, every eligible voter gets two votes, one for a candidate who represents their electorate and another for the party that they want to represent them.

Electorate Vote

The electorate vote is similar to the first-past-the-post system, where the candidate with the most votes is elected to represent their electorate, which roughly has a population of 75,000 people. Crucially, not all of the seats in parliament are electorate seats, and a minimum of 48 are elected from the party vote.

Party Vote

The party vote can be a bit more difficult to understand, so I'll try and make this easy to follow.

Your party vote decides the proportion of the 120 seats that each party recieves. In our current system, for a party to be represented in the house they must pass the electoral threshold. In order to pass the threshold, a party must either:

  • Recieve 5% of the total party vote, or
  • Win an electorate seat

When they pass the threshold, a party is entitled to their share of the seats based on the proportion of the party vote that they recieved (excluding the parties that did not pass the threshold). I'll use the 2020 election as an example:
