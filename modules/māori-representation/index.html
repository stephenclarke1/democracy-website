Māori Representation

Māori have always had a unique relationship with the Pākehā system of governance that Aotearoa now has.

History

In 1840, more than 500 Māori rangatira signed te Tiriti o Waitangi (The Treaty of Waitangi), an agreement between tangata whenua and the British crown. There are two versions of the treaty, one in English and one in te reo Māori, which means there are some not insignificant differences with the phrasing and language used.

To paraphrase 183 years of conflict and debate around how to interperet this document, the European version said that the Crown would get sovereignty over the land, ‘exclusive and undisturbed possession' of Māori land, while the te reo Māori version says that the crown would get governance over the land, and that Māori would be garanteed tino rangatiratanga - full rights of cheiftanship over their whenua and taonga.

Māori Electorates

There are now seven Māori electorates, which each correspond to one seat in Parliament. These are:

  • Hauraki-Waikato – north-western North Island, including Hamilton and Papakura
  • Ikaroa-Rāwhiti – eastern and southern North Island, including Gisborne and Masterton
  • Tāmaki Makaurau – southern and central Auckland, and parts of western Auckland
  • Te Tai Hauāuru – western North Island, including Taranaki and Manawatū-Whanganui regions
  • Te Tai Tokerau – northernmost seat, including Whangārei, northern Auckland and parts of western Auckland
  • Te Tai Tonga – all of the South Island, Stewart Island / Rakiura, Chatham Islands, and most of Wellington
    • Waiariki – includes Tauranga, Whakatāne, Rotorua, Taupō

The number of Māori registered on the Māori roll determines how many Māori electorates there are.

Māori Roll

Māori have the choice to either be on the Māori roll or the general roll, which determines whether your electorate vote is for the general electorate or Māori electorate you live in.

Under the current legislation, Māori can choose to switch to the other roll if they want to at any time unless there are three months until an election (local or national). Before this legislation came about in 2023, Māori could choose when they first enrolled, then would only be able to change rolls once every five years.
