Democracy in Aotearoa

Democracy is how Aotearoa New Zealand gets things done. It is important for a nation to have a system of democracy that represents people, so that the descisions made by those in power reflect the attitudes of the majority of the population.

The House of Representatives

It's probably the room you think of when you think about parliament (if you do at all), but what is it?

In order to represent New Zealand's views, 120 people are elected to the House of Representatives, more commonly known as Parliament. 72 of those people are representatives of a particular area, or electorate. Of these 72 electorates, seven of them are M훮ori electorates, and have been elected by people of M훮ori descent to represent their views. Read more about the M훮ori electorates.

The other 48 Members of Parliament (or MPs) have been elected by the whole country, as part of the party vote, which you will learn more about later.

Government

When a particular party gets over 60 seats in the House, which is conveniently half of the total number of seats, they become the government.

Generally in MMP, not one party gets all of the votes, and parties have to work together to form coalitions in order to make up the numbers to govern. In the 2020 general election however, the Labour Party achieved the highest proportion of seats ever under MMP in Aotearoa, winning 64, so were able to govern alone, without the support of another party.
